# Value Investing -- Theoretical Foundation

## One-Line Summary

**"Price is what you pay, value is what you get."** A strategy of estimating a company's *intrinsic value*, buying it at a sufficient discount to market price (margin of safety), and waiting until the market re-recognizes its value. This is an approach that *tries to beat* the market, so it is the polar opposite of Bogleheads.

## Theoretical Origins

- **Benjamin Graham** (1894–1976) — *Security Analysis* (1934), *The Intelligent Investor* (1949). Professor at Columbia Business School. The father of modern value investing.
- **Warren Buffett** — Graham's disciple. Proved the method empirically with Berkshire Hathaway. In his later period, under the influence of Philip Fisher and Munger, evolved toward "Quality Value."
- **Charlie Munger** — Preferred "a wonderful company at a fair price" over "a fair company at a wonderful price."
- **Joel Greenblatt** — *Magic Formula* (a simplified quantitative value-investing approach based on ROIC × Earnings Yield).

## Core Concepts

### 1. Intrinsic Value

- The present value of the sum of future cash flows a company will generate
- Essentially an *estimate*, so *conservative estimation* matters more than precision
- Estimation tools: DCF, Dividend Discount Model, liquidation value, P/B·P/E comparison

### 2. Margin of Safety

- Buy only at a 30–50% discount to intrinsic value
- *Insurance against estimation error*
- Graham: "If you buy 100 won of value for 60 won, 40 won is on your side"

### 3. Mr. Market

- A metaphor for the market as a manic-depressive business partner
- Some days he offers to buy expensively, other days he begs to sell cheaply
- **You have the freedom to *accept or ignore* his offers**
- Don't be swayed by market sentiment

### 4. Circle of Competence

- Buffett: Don't invest in businesses you don't understand
- *Knowing the boundary* matters more than expanding it
- The reason Buffett avoided tech stocks in the 1990s

### 5. Economic Moat

- A sustainable competitive advantage (brand, network, switching costs, scale, patents)
- Morningstar: Wide moat / Narrow moat / No moat
- Core of Buffett's later investment style

### 6. Quantitative Filters
- PER (price-to-earnings ratio) < 15
- PBR (price-to-book ratio) < 1.5
- ROE > 15% sustained
- Low debt ratio
- Greenblatt Magic Formula: top ROIC ∩ top Earnings Yield
